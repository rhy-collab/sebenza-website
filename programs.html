Who is IRAAC?

IRAAC is the Aboriginal Community Organisation at the centre of this body of work, and the client Sebenza's IRAAC-facing proposals are built for. It operates under the NSW Local Decision Making framework and is led by a Board, with Geoff Maher as Chairperson and Maria Maher as Secretary. IRAAC runs four community programs — MCC, YouthScape, The Crew and DARC — and is investing in its own governance, reporting and administrative systems so it can demonstrate to funders and regulators that it is well run.

A big part of IRAAC's ambition reaches beyond its own operations: it wants to eventually support other Aboriginal Community Organisations the same way, sharing the systems and training it builds for itself. That ambition is still taking shape — IRAAC is a young, still-forming organisation, and much of its own public-facing story is still being written.

Programs we support

Each program below is delivered by IRAAC and backed by the governance, funding and reporting systems Sebenza builds alongside it.

Governance capability

MCC — Mob and Country Connections

IRAAC's own program for helping other Aboriginal Community Organisations build governance, administrative and reporting capability, using the systems and training IRAAC has already developed for itself. It's described as an Aboriginal-led governance capability initiative, and doubles as a pilot for testing whether peer-to-peer capability building genuinely improves outcomes across the wider sector.

Support through MCC is only ever given at the invitation of the organisation receiving it — relationship-based rather than top-down. In effect, MCC is IRAAC working to become a capability hub for smaller Aboriginal organisations across the sector, not just a program it runs for itself.

Youth

YouthScape

A program connecting young people in community with culture, opportunity and support, backed by the same reporting and governance foundation as Sebenza's other work with IRAAC. Like IRAAC's other programs, its detailed activities and outcomes are still being documented publicly — this is an early-stage program rather than a fully mature, publicly-documented one yet.

Community

The Crew

A community-facing program focused on building skills, connection and participation more broadly, run through IRAAC and supported by Sebenza's funding, reporting and administrative frameworks. As with YouthScape, the specific activities and the people it supports are still being fleshed out in IRAAC's own public materials.

Community

DARC

The fourth of IRAAC's programs, working alongside MCC, YouthScape and The Crew to support community outcomes. Less detail is available on DARC than IRAAC's other three programs — it's best understood, for now, as a named program still awaiting fuller public documentation, including its full name.

The wider governance ecosystem

IRAAC and Sebenza don't operate in isolation — everything sits inside a wider system of government policy, regional participation structures and compliance regulators. Understanding how they connect makes it easier to see why governance, reporting and funding work the way it does.

NSW Government

Aboriginal Affairs NSW

The NSW Government agency responsible for Aboriginal affairs policy, and the government body IRAAC and Sebenza's reporting ultimately answers to. It oversees the OCHRE policy framework and the Local Decision Making framework, both of which shape how organisations like IRAAC operate — holding Ministerial advice, Treasury accountability, policy leadership, funding assurance and compliance oversight. It's the senior government partner that funds and regulates the whole ecosystem these other entities sit inside, and the agency Sebenza's governance options paper was originally prepared for.

Proposed coordination

NCARA

A proposed or potential statewide Aboriginal coordinating structure, sitting somewhere between Aboriginal Affairs NSW and the regional Alliances and Assemblies. Its possible role is to provide coordination, shared learning and a representative voice across the sector, rather than exercising direct control over any single organisation. NCARA's exact function isn't settled yet — one risk worth naming honestly is that it could end up duplicating what Alliances and Assemblies already do if its role isn't clearly defined. Think of it less as an existing agency and more as a structure being floated for how the sector could organise itself in future.

Regional participation

Alliances & Assemblies

The regional participation structures that sit underneath Aboriginal Affairs NSW within the Local Decision Making framework, rather than single named organisations. Alliances are regional Aboriginal governance structures that support engagement, priority identification and planning; Assemblies are community forums or representative structures that support local participation and legitimacy. Together they form the "participatory architecture" through which Aboriginal communities influence priorities and planning under LDM — and IRAAC works alongside them to progress community priorities.

Corporate regulator

ORIC — Office of the Registrar of Indigenous Corporations

One of the two regulators IRAAC reports to as a registered Aboriginal Community Organisation, covering the corporate compliance side of things — quarterly reports, annual reports and audits. Every Sebenza proposal that touches governance training or reporting infrastructure explicitly accounts for ORIC's expectations. It's a national-level regulator rather than a state government body, sitting outside the Aboriginal Affairs NSW and Local Decision Making system — one of the compliance boxes IRAAC has to keep ticking to stay in good standing as a corporation.

Charity regulator

ACNC — Australian Charities and Not-for-profits Commission

The other main regulator alongside ORIC, covering the charity side of IRAAC's compliance obligations rather than the corporate side. Like ORIC, it shows up mainly in reporting calendars, training requirements and the governance systems Sebenza builds for IRAAC — the two regulators are almost always mentioned together, since IRAAC needs to satisfy both simultaneously. It's a federal body rather than a NSW one, so it operates independently of the Aboriginal Affairs NSW relationship. Keeping both ORIC and ACNC happy is treated as a basic proof point of good governance for funders.

Policy framework

OCHRE & Local Decision Making (LDM)

Not organisations in the traditional sense, but the shared policy backdrop everything else operates inside. OCHRE is Aboriginal Affairs NSW's overarching policy framework for Aboriginal affairs in the state; Local Decision Making is the specific framework intended to give Aboriginal communities a stronger say in decisions that affect them. IRAAC, Aboriginal Affairs NSW, NCARA, the Alliances and Assemblies, and IRAAC's programs all sit inside this framework — which is why Sebenza's governance work spends so much time exploring how much decision-making power should move from the centre out to the regional and community level.
